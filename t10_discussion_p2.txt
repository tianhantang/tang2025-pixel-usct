#discussion/limitation
\label{sec:limitation}

The pixel-based imaging method relies on creating a dictionary for each pixel using a linear acoustic model, which is built upon the assumptions stated in Section 2.1.
However, these assumptions are not entirely valid in practical applications, and their effects are addressed here.

First, while attenuation inhomogeneity primarily affects the amplitude of each pixel's ``code'' in the dictionary, it is effectively handled by the least-squares solution in [@eq:pixel-based-inverse].
In contrast, sound speed inhomogeneity poses a significant challenge as it impacts the positions of the codes in the dictionary.
The current method struggles in this regard, leading to abrupt deviations in the reconstructed echogenicity map when sound speed inhomogeneities are present.
Synthetic aperture is also affected by sound speed inhomogeneities---as it assumes a constant speed during reconstruction---however, it is generally less sensitive to this issue.
SA method typically suffers a loss of spatial resolution due to the ``out-of-focus'' effect, but still preserves the overall structure of the target.
To mitigate sound speed inhomogeneity in the proposed method, one potential strategy is sound speed sweeping: precomputing multiple $\mathbf{G}$ matrices corresponding to a range of sound speeds, performing parallel reconstructions for each, and selecting the best result.
Another promising approach leverages the intrinsic nature of USCT: using the pass-through wave to construct a sound speed map and generating a tailored $\mathbf{G}$ matrix based on it.

Second, the assumption of isotropic scattering is generally valid for soft tissue imaging.
While strong scatterer directivity may influence the optimal configuration for specific targets, the weak directivity typical in soft tissues is unlikely to cause significant issues.
The least-squares method effectively compensates for variations in code amplitude caused by weak directivity.

Third, the use of Born's approximation is a widely accepted practice in soft tissue imaging and should not introduce significant errors.

Fourth, excluding the pass-through signals in the simulation is justified as these signals primarily overlap with the scattering signals along the line connecting the TX/RX elements.
Theoretically, pass-through signals can be separated from real data in time domain and removed without substantial information loss.
Moreover, in practice, acquisition schemes can be designed to strictly use only the reflection signals for reconstruction, as demonstrated in [@cite:Qu-2016].

Fifth, the background acoustic artifacts, such as reflections from the transducer element surfaces, pose another challenge in real-world applications.
These artifacts are not accounted for in the $\mathbf{G}$ matrix model and must be removed from raw RF data during preprocessing.
For sparse objects (e.g., wire phantoms), subtraction of a water-only dataset can effectively eliminate these reflections.
For denser targets, such as human breast, attenuation effects may naturally diminish their impact.

Additionally, for real-scale breast imaging, as stated in Section 2.2, the large number of pixels and data samples will result in a tremendous $\mathbf{G}$ matrix, with size being several orders of magnitude larger than that used in the simulation studies.
This makes the pixel-based method which solves the inverse problem far more computationally intensive than synthetic aperture reconstruction at the same scale.

Moving forward, we aim to apply the pixel-based USCT echo imaging method to experiment data, focusing on addressing inhomogeneous sound speed issue in biological media, as well as dealing with acoustic artifacts from actual systems.
