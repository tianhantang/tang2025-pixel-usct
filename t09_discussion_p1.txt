#discussion/pixel-method
\label{sec:advantage-and-feature}

The pixel-based imaging method offers spatial resolution comparable to the synthetic aperture method, with much higher contrast ratio, while requiring significantly fewer elements.
For instance, a 16-element system employing the pixel-based method outperforms a 128-element system using the SA method, which implies an 8-fold reduction in hardware complexity.
Moreover, this is a 64-fold reduction in the raw dataset, significantly alleviating data transfer and storage bottlenecks.

The advantages of the pixel-based imaging method over the synthetic aperture method can be understood by inspecting the mathematical form of the reconstruction process.
From an algebraic perspective, the reconstruction process of the synthetic aperture can also be expressed in a matrix form by [@eq:synthetic-aperture-matrix]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:synthetic-aperture-matrix}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:selection-matrix}
\mathbf{a} = \vert \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{\tilde{y}} \vert
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pre-processed-y}
\mathbf{\tilde{y}} = \mathcal{H} \big( \mathrm{TGC}(\mathbf{y}) \big)
\end{equation}
\end{subequations}
<!----------->
where $\mathbf{a}$ is 1-D column vector of the uncompressed pixel values;
$\mathbf{\tilde{y}}$ is the preprocessed RF data from all TX/RX pairs concatenated into a 1-D column vector;
$\mathbf{S}$ is the ``selection'' matrix, whose entries are either 0 or 1, that performs the pickup-and-sum operation as detailed in [@eq:synthetic-aperture].
Inserting $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{G} \mathbf{x}$ into [@eq:synthetic-aperture-matrix] will not recreate $\mathbf{x}$.
In contrast, the pixel-based imaging method simulates the measurement matrix (which includes not only travel time, but also attenuation, energy spreading, and waveform), allowing solving the problem of [@eq:01] by pseudo-inverse of $\mathbf{G}$, thus recreating $\mathbf{x}$.

Additionally, the proposed method is as versatile as the synthetic aperture method, being equally applicable to non-ring-shaped systems and non-point-source acquisition schemes---such as those described in [@cite:Gemmeke-KIT-3D-USCT-2017] and [@cite:QT-USCT-System-2011]---by constructing the proper $\mathbf{G}$ matrices.
