Comparisons between the reconstructions from the pixel-based imaging method and the synthetic aperture method (SA) are shown in [@fig:compare-resolution] and [@fig:compare-contrast].
The SNR of the RF data for reconstruction is 40 dB.

[@fig:compare-resolution] compares the spatial resolution of SA and the pixel-based method using the same scoring phantom as in [@fig:reconst-snr].
For SA, a full 128-element or uniformly configured 64-element system reveals all the phantom's features;
However, reducing the number of elements to 32 or fewer results in a loss of visual sharpness and details.
In contrast, the pixel-based imaging method shows no visually identifiable degradation when reducing the number of elements from 128 to 64, 32, and even 16, maintaining comparable resolution to that of SA using a full system;
When reduced to 8 elements, there are perceptible speckles and a decline in brightness;
However, reducing the number of elements to 4 results in a complete collapse of the image.

The bottom right portion of the phantom, which contains several closely spaced point scatterers, is used for the quantitative evaluation.
Two representative line profiles, denoted by the red dashed lines in each zoom-in figure---one from SA using a full system and the other from the pixel-based method using only a 16-element system---are plotted.
Both results exhibit single-pixel level (0.5 mm) resolution, as the pixel value of each point scatterer outweighs the adjacent background by more than 3 dB.

[@fig:compare-contrast] compares the contrast of SA and the pixel-based method using a female breast-mimicking phantom, which models skin, fat, mammary tissue, and a tumor.
The echogenicity values for these tissues are set to $-0.11$, $-0.01$, $-0.06$, and $-0.03$, respectively, which are estimations based on reflection coefficients calculated from acoustic parameters in [@cite:Swiss-ITIS-database] and [@cite:Nebeker-2012], along with empirical observations: skin exhibits high echogenicity, fat is low, mammary tissue is moderate, and tumors can appear either brighter or darker in B-mode images.
For SA, both a full 128-element system and a uniformly configured 64-element system clearly reveal all phantom structures.
However, reducing the element count to 32 and below results in a loss of structural information and contrast.
For the pixel-based imaging method, reducing the element count from 128 to 64 or 32 produces no visually significant degradation, with images maintaining much higher contrast than SA.
At 16 elements, slight speckling appears in the mammary tissue, but the overall image remains clear and sharp. 
At 8 elements, speckling becomes more pronounced, though structures remain recognizable.
At 4 elements, the image collapses entirely, failing to reveal any internal structures.

We evaluate the contrast quantitatively by calculating the contrast ratio (CR) between the tumor and the mammary tissue using [@eq:compute-contrast]:
<!-- latext -->
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:compute-contrast}
\mathrm{CR} = 20 * \log_{10} \frac{ \vert \mathrm{Mean}(X_1) - \mathrm{Mean}(X_2) \vert }{ \sqrt{ \mathrm{Std}(X_1)^2 + \mathrm{Std}(X_2)^2 } }
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
<!---->
where, $X_1$ is the group of pixels inside the tumor region; $X_2$ is the group of pixels inside the mammary tissue region.
The result shows that the pixel-based imaging method using only 8 or 16 elements is good enough that outperforms SA using a full or 64-element system.
