In this study, we conduct simulations to investigate the characteristics of the pixel-based echo imaging method and assess the practicality of our strategy for minimizing data acquisition.
We also compare the performance of our method with that of the conventional synthetic aperture method.

<!-- ### CONDITION -->
To maintain computational efficiency, we use a 1/16 scaled-down model of the actual system depicted in [@fig:01], with detailed simulation parameters provided in [@tb:01].

The simulation is performed in time domain within 2D space.
We model the acoustic source as a time-varying pressure point located at the center of the transducer element surface, radiating acoustic energy uniformly in all directions, without applying any directivity.
The source employs a 4-cycle Gaussian-modulated sinusoid wave with a peak amplitude of $p_0$.
Adhering to the principle of energy conservation, the finite size of the transmission element---denoted as $L_{e}$, representing the element width in 2D simulation---is considered, establishing key relationships between power ($P$), intensity ($I$), and pressure ($p$), as outlined in [@eq:power-intensity-pressure-relation]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:power-intensity-pressure-relation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:power-intensity-relation}
I = \frac{P}{L_{e}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:intensity-pressure-relation}
I \propto p^{2}
\end{equation}
\end{subequations}
<!----------->

For a scatterer at a distance $D_{TX}$ from the transmission element, the relationship between the transmission and the incident wave pressure---denoted as $p_{0}$ and $p_{i}$ correspondingly---is described by [@eq:transmission-incident-relation]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:transmission-incident-relation}
p_{0}^{2} \cdot L_{e} = 2\pi \cdot D_{TX} \cdot p_{i}^{2}
\end{equation}
<!----------->
Upon scattering, the incident wave pressure $p_{i}$ and the scattering wave pressure $p_{s}$ are related by the echogenicity $\chi$ in [@eq:incident-scattering-relation]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:incident-scattering-relation}
p_{s}^2 = \vert\chi\vert \cdot p_{i}^{2}
\end{equation}
<!----------->
Here, $\vert\chi\vert$ denotes the fraction of incident acoustic power being scattered, therefore $\vert\chi\vert \le 1$.
Negative echogenicity implies a 180-degree phase shift in the scattering wave relative to the incident wave, because echogenicity is a result of the acoustic impedance difference among media---when the incident wave travels from high to low impedance media, phase inversion happens.
Unit echogenicity ($\chi = 1$) is a hypothetical case, used in generating the $\mathbf{G}$ matrix.
<!-- @note: consider the scatterer as a disk having different acoustic impedance from the surroundings. -->

The scatterer acts as an acoustic source in the scattering process.
Each scatterer is assumed to be contained within a pixel, assigned a ``cross-length'' (a 2D adaptation of cross-section) equal to the pixel size $d$, enabling the calculation of received wave pressure $p_{r}$ at an element located $D_{RX}$ away, as described in [@eq:scattering-reception-relation]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:scattering-reception-relation}
p_{s}^{2} \cdot d = 2\pi \cdot D_{RX} \cdot p_{r}^{2}
\end{equation}
<!----------->

Through back-substitution, we elucidate the relationship between the received wave pressure and the transmission wave pressure, incorporating scatterer location and echogenicity, as shown in [@eq:transmission-reception-relation]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:transmission-reception-relation}
p_{r} & = p_s \sqrt{d/(2\pi \cdot D_{Rx})} \\
      & =  \operatorname{sgn}(\chi) \cdot p_i \sqrt{\vert\chi\vert \cdot d/(2\pi \cdot D_{Rx})} \\
      & =  \operatorname{sgn}(\chi) \cdot p_0 \sqrt{\vert\chi\vert \cdot (L_e \cdot d) / (D_{TX} \cdot D_{RX})} / (2\pi)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
<!----------->

Furthermore, we account for ultrasound wave attenuation using the power law model---as widely employed in many simulation studies [@cite:Verweij-Ultrasound-Simulation-2014]---as described in [@eq:power-law]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:power-law}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:power-law-TX}
20 \cdot \log_{10} \big( \frac{p(\mathbf{s})}{p(\mathbf{0})} \big) = -\alpha_0 \cdot f_{c} \cdot D_{TX}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:power-law-RX}
20 \cdot \log_{10} \big( \frac{p(\mathbf{r})}{p(\mathbf{s})} \big) = -\alpha_0 \cdot f_{c} \cdot D_{RX}
\end{equation}
\end{subequations}
where $p(\mathbf{0})$, $p(\mathbf{s})$, and $p(\mathbf{r})$ represent acoustic pressure at the source point, the scatterer point, and the receiving element point respectively as if there were no energy spreading.

<!----------->
Incorporating attenuation effects into our model refines the expression for the received wave pressure, as shown in [@eq:transmission-attenuation-reception-relation]:
<!-- latex -->
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:transmission-attenuation-reception-relation}
p_{r} = {} & \operatorname{sgn}(\chi) \cdot \sqrt{\vert\chi\vert \cdot (L_e \cdot d) / (D_{TX} \cdot D_{RX})}\\
& \cdot 10^{-\alpha_0 \cdot f_{c} \cdot (D_{TX} + D_{RX}) / 20} \cdot p_{0} / (2\pi) 
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
<!----------->

The simulation experiments start by investigating the condition numbers for systems with different element counts, uniformities, and positions, as categorized in [@fig:03].
This exploration aims to understand how these factors affect the condition number, which is an indicator of measurement robustness to noise.
We compute the condition number ($\kappa$) by its definition, utilizing singular value decomposition (SVD), as shown in [@eq:condition-number-definition]:
<!-- latext -->
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:condition-number-definition}
\kappa(\mathbf{G}) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf{G})}{\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{G})}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
<!---->
where $\sigma_{\max}(\mathbf{G})$ and $\sigma_{\min}(\mathbf{G})$ are the largest and smallest singular values of $\mathbf{G}$ respectively.
We only examine systems that have full column rank, as it is a prerequisite stated in Section 2.2.
The configurations of those systems are listed in [@tb:system-confs].

Next, we verify the practicality of the condition number as a measurement performance indicator by conducting simulations on various ``scoring'' phantoms, using systems with different condition numbers, under varying noise levels.
These phantoms feature distinctive patterns, facilitating visual assessment and pixel-to-pixel error analysis.
To incorporate random thermal noise effect, we introduce a normally distributed noise component into our model, resulting in [@eq:linear-model-w-noise]:
<!-- latext -->
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:linear-model-w-noise}
\mathbf{G x + n = y}
\end{equation}
<!---->
where $\mathbf{n}$ represents the noise, and $\mathbf{y}$ denotes the acquired noisy data.

Finally, we compare the performance of the proposed method against the synthetic aperture method in terms of spatial resolution and contrast.
For a given RF dataset with a specific SNR, and for each system with a specific configuration, we perform reconstructions using both the pixel-based method and the synthetic aperture method.

For the synthetic aperture method, we perform beamforming at each pixel and summing across signals from all the TX/RX pairs coherently, as expressed in [@eq:synthetic-aperture] [@cite:Treeby-kWave-2010]:
<!-- latext -->
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:synthetic-aperture}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:synthetic-aperture-beamforming}
A(i,j) = \Big\vert \sum_{m=1}^{N_{TX}} \sum_{n=1}^{N_{RX}} \mathcal{H} \big( \mathrm{TGC}(y_{m,n}) \big)(\tau) \Big\vert
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:two-way-traval-time}
\tau = \frac{D_{TX} + D_{RX}}{c_0}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:log-compression}
I = \frac{\log_{10}\left(1 + a \cdot A / \max(A)\right)}{\log_{10}(1 + a)}
\end{equation}
\end{subequations}
<!---->
where, $\mathcal{H}$ denotes the Hilbert Transform;
$y_{m,n}$ denotes the RF signal of the $n$-th RX channel from the $m$-th TX event;
$\mathrm{TGC}$ denotes the time-gain-compensation.
$A(i, j)$ denotes the uncompressed signal strength at the $i$-th row $j$-th column pixel;
$I$ denotes the logarithm-compressed image---note it is already normalized in range $[0, 1]$---and it is then mapped to 8-bit grayscale image for display.
The parameter $a$ controls the compression curve and implicitly determines the dynamic range.
In this study, $a$ is set to $-0.7$, corresponding to an effective dynamic range of approximately 43.5 dB.

For the proposed method, we first solve the inverse problem for $\mathbf{x}$, and restore the echogenicity vector $\chi$, then assign the relative strength to each corresponding pixel, and reshape the result into 2D format, as expressed in [@eq:pixel-based-method]:
<!-- latext -->
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:pixel-based-method}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pixel-based-inverse}
\mathbf{x} = \arg \min_{\mathbf{x}} \lVert \mathbf{y-Gx} \rVert_2^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pixel-based-post}
\chi = \operatorname{sgn}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{x}^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pixel-based-rescale}
I = \operatorname{Reshape}\Big( \frac{\vert \chi \vert}{\max(\vert \chi \vert)} \Big)
\end{equation}
\end{subequations}
<!---->
where $I$ denotes the reconstructed image in range $[0, 1]$---unlike synthetic aperture, no logarithm compression is applied---and it is linearly mapped to 8-bit image for display.
Note that [@eq:pixel-based-post] is necessary since $\chi$ is under the square root in the forward process model of [@eq:transmission-attenuation-reception-relation].
